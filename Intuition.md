# Is Technical Analysis a Science or a Collection of Observations?

## The short, honest answer

Technical analysis (TA) is **not a science in the strict sense**. It is a disciplined empirical craft — a mix of observed regularities in crowd behavior, statistical techniques, and behavioral assumptions. Whether it "works" is genuinely contested: academics have historically dismissed it, working traders defend it, and recent statistical research finds *some* rules survive rigorous testing in *some* markets.

The honest framing: **TA is a framework for reading crowd behavior through price and volume, whose edge is real but smaller, more conditional, and more fragile than its marketing suggests.**

---

## 1. The three assumptions TA is built on

Every technical analyst, knowingly or not, accepts three premises that go back to Charles Dow (founder of the Wall Street Journal, writing around 1900):

1. **"The market discounts everything."** All known information — earnings, news, sentiment, macro data — is already baked into price. You don't need to study the company; you study the price. This is [consistent with the efficient market hypothesis](https://www.strike.money/technical-analysis/dow-theory).

2. **Prices move in trends.** Markets experience [primary trends lasting one to several years, secondary corrections against them, and minor trends of market noise lasting days to weeks](https://www.litefinance.org/blog/for-beginners/technical-analysis/dow-theory/). A trend is assumed to continue until there is evidence it has reversed.

3. **History repeats itself** — because human psychology (fear, greed, hope) is roughly constant, the patterns those emotions produce in price charts tend to recur.

These are **working assumptions**, not provable theorems. That distinction matters for the science question.

---

## 2. Why it *could* work — the mechanisms

Three plausible mechanisms, not mutually exclusive:

### a) Price genuinely contains information
If markets aggregate what buyers and sellers collectively know, price action and volume are a compressed signal of that consensus. Studying them is studying the footprint of informed money.

### b) Behavioral finance: humans are predictably irrational
Overreaction, underreaction, anchoring, herding, and loss aversion produce recurring patterns. Academic research links TA's success to documented anomalies — [the success of technical trading rules is associated with asset pricing anomalies such as momentum and reversal](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1811.06766). These are well-established effects in peer-reviewed finance.

### c) Self-fulfilling prophecy
If enough traders watch the same 200-day moving average or the same support level, [their collective actions can create a feedback loop where market prices move in the direction predicted by the analysis](https://www.lbank.com/questions/ardnsw1742787871). A support level "holds" partly because thousands of traders place buy orders there *because* it was the support level.

The honest counter: [for the self-fulfilling prophecy to fully hold, all market participants would need to agree on the same time frame, the same pattern interpretation, and execute the same way — which isn't how markets actually operate](https://www.allstarcharts.com/education/criticisms-technical-analysis/technical-analysis-self-fulfilling-prophecy). So "self-fulfilling" is a partial mechanism, not a complete explanation.

---

## 3. The case against — why academics are skeptical

The critique is serious and worth hearing clearly:

- **The random walk argument.** [Academics largely see technical analysis as pseudoscientific. Efficient market theorist Burton Malkiel argues stock prices are random and that investors relying on TA accomplish nothing but increasing brokerage charges. Warren Buffett said he realized TA didn't work when he turned the chart upside down and didn't get a different answer. Peter Lynch quipped that charts are great for predicting the past](https://www.irishtimes.com/business/personal-finance/charting-the-stock-markets-does-technical-analysis-work-1.3148453).

- **Curve-fitting.** [One can always describe a curve through a set of points, even random points, to any arbitrary degree of accuracy — this will not predict future behavior unless the curve describes a real physical system](https://theness.com/stock-market-pseudoscience/). Backtesting thousands of indicator combinations almost guarantees finding something that "worked" in the past by pure chance.

- **Subjectivity.** The same chart shown to five analysts produces five interpretations. A head-and-shoulders is obvious in hindsight and ambiguous in real time. Scientific facts don't behave this way.

- **Data mining bias.** [Testing a large set of technical trading rules on a given data set risks false rejections of market efficiency, as some rules in a large set will, by chance, prove to be profitable ex post](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1811.06766). Modern research uses multiple-hypothesis-testing frameworks to filter out these false positives.

---

## 4. What rigorous research actually finds

More nuanced than either camp admits:

- Early academic work (Brock et al., 1992) found simple rules like moving-average crossovers had statistically significant predictive power.
- Later work (Sullivan et al., 1999; Bajgrowicz & Scaillet, 2012) showed much of that edge disappears once you correct for data-snooping bias and transaction costs.
- More recent studies using stricter statistical controls find the [literature is split between studies highlighting genuine profitability of technical analysis and those arguing against it](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1811.06766). Some rules survive; many don't.
- TA appears to work better in **less efficient markets** (forex, commodities, emerging markets, crypto) than in highly liquid large-cap equities.
- [Up to 40 percent of foreign exchange traders see technical analysis as important for predicting price action over short time horizons](https://www.irishtimes.com/business/personal-finance/charting-the-stock-markets-does-technical-analysis-work-1.3148453) — which itself is a reason TA has real effects in FX.

---

## 5. So — science or collection of observations?

| Question | Verdict |
|---|---|
| Is it a hard science like physics? | **No.** Results aren't reproducible in the strict sense; interpretation is subjective. |
| Is it pure superstition? | **Also no.** It rests on documented behavioral anomalies and sometimes survives rigorous statistical testing. |
| What is it, then? | **A disciplined empirical craft** — a framework of observed regularities in crowd behavior, expressed through price and volume, validated through experience and partially through statistics. |

A useful analogy: TA is like **medicine before the randomized controlled trial era**. A body of practical knowledge accumulated through careful observation, some of it genuinely true, some of it folklore. The practitioner's skill lies in telling the two apart.

My job as I learn isn't to treat every pattern as gospel — it's to develop judgment about *which* patterns have real behavioral logic, *under what market conditions* they work, and *where* they fail.

---

## Framework for learning each concept going forward

For every pattern or indicator I study, I will answer three questions:

1. **What's the behavioral story?** Why would humans produce this pattern?
2. **What does the evidence say?** Does it survive testing, or is it folklore?
3. **When does it fail?** Every pattern has a regime where it breaks.

This builds working intuition rather than a checklist.
